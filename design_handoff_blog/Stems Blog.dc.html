A website that says you grow "seasonal British flowers" and was last touched in March isn't helping anyone in July.

What you need is much smaller and much more useful: one clear place that shows what you've got this week, with prices, that you can keep current in a few minutes and share with a single link.

Most small growers already have an audience. It's just scattered.

  • Instagram is where the photos and the goodwill live. It's brilliant for showing the field and terrible for "ranunculus, 60p a stem, ready Friday".
  • Your local florists are your best repeat buyers. They want local stock and a story to tell their own customers, and they'd rather text one grower than trawl an auction.
  • Markets and the farm gate bring the public who want a bucket of just-cut stems over a bunch flown in from the other side of the world.
  • Word of mouth in your area, especially through other growers, does more than any ad ever will.

Make your availability easy to share

Here's the pattern that works, however you do it technically.

  1. Keep one list, in one place. Variety, colour, price per stem, and whether it's ready now or coming soon. One source of truth, not twelve different answers in twelve different chats.
  2. Update it as you cut. A list is only worth sharing if people trust it's current. Touch it when the picture changes, even just to say what's finished.
  3. Share the same link every time. Put that link at the top of your Instagram, in your bio, in your email signature, and on a sign at the market.

Buyers can find you by name or area without signing in, and when they want your flowers they contact you directly, the way they always have. You don't need a website. You need one current list of what you've got, a single link to share it, and to be findable by the people looking for local flowers. Get those three right and selling locally gets a lot quieter, in the good way.
