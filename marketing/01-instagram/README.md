# 01: Instagram

The primary channel. Read `../00-foundations/positioning.md` and
`../00-foundations/social-voice.md` before you write anything here. The social
voice doc is the law for everything in this folder.

## Why this channel

Instagram is where the growers already are. It is the one place nearly every
small British flower grower has a presence, posts their work, and fields "is the
dahlia still going?" DMs one at a time. It is also where a grower's Stems link
lives: top of profile, in the bio, on a Story sticker.

So our account is doing two jobs at once:

1. **Reaching growers directly** (acquisition). Other growers are the people most
   likely to admire a grower's setup, and the ones we most want to sign up.
2. **Modelling what a grower's own Stems-linked posts can look like.** When a
   grower sees our feed, they should think "I could post like that, and my page
   link would sit right there." We show, we don't pitch.

This is not a brand awareness channel for buyers. Buyers arrive because a grower
they already follow shared a link. We are talking to growers, in the room where
growers are.

## Who we reach

- Small and freelance British flower growers, already on Instagram, already
  posting beautiful photos with no good way to show live availability.
- The wider British-grown / slow-flowers community who will recognise and share
  good seasonal content.
- Secondarily, the florists and keen buyers who follow growers and the movement.

## What good looks like

The only number that matters is growers with a live, shared Stems page (see
`positioning.md`). On Instagram specifically, the leading signals are:

- Growers replying to or DMing us "how do I get one of these pages?"
- Featured growers resharing their feature to their own audience.
- Saves and shares on "in season now" posts (a quieter signal that the content
  is genuinely useful, not that we chased it).

We do not chase follower count or likes. Calm is the brand. A small, real,
grower-respected account beats a big noisy one.

## The plan

A simple, sustainable rhythm a solo founder can actually keep in season:

- **2 to 3 posts a week.** Most of them grower features. The feed is mostly other
  people's flowers, credited.
- **Stories most days when there's something real** to show: a grower's cutting
  video reshared with permission, a page being updated on a phone in a polytunnel,
  a market stall going up.
- **One "in season now" post a week** through the British growing season
  (roughly March to October), lighter in winter (anemones, ranunculus, forced
  bulbs, foliage, dried).
- **Reply to everything** like a person, by name. Comments and DMs are where the
  acquisition actually happens.

### The link-in-bio mechanic

This is the whole point, and it is the thing we are teaching growers to copy.

- Our own bio link points to `/discover` (the public discovery page).
- Every grower feature names the grower and points people to their
  `stems.market/@handle`. We are showing, post by post, that "the link in the
  bio is where this week's stock lives."
- In Stories, use the link sticker honestly: "@somegrower's stock this week"
  linking to their page, never "SIGN UP NOW".
- The implicit lesson for every grower watching: your Stems link belongs at the
  top of your profile, and you point people to it instead of re-typing your list
  into twelve DMs.

## Tactics specific to the flower-grower world

### Grower features (most of the feed)

- A photo (theirs, with permission, credited) plus one or two lines: who they
  are, where, what's in this week, their `@handle`.
- Always tag the grower and, where it fits, their location. Credit generously.
- Rotate across regions and scales: a Devon field, a Yorkshire allotment plot, a
  back-garden cutting patch. Show the range of who belongs here.
- Ask permission every time. Save the permission (a screenshotted "yes please")
  so there is never any doubt.

### "In season now" posts

- What is actually coming into British fields this week or month. Name varieties.
- These position Stems as a calm seasonal authority and are the most shareable
  evergreen-ish content we have.
- Accuracy matters. If you are unsure when something is genuinely in season in
  the UK, check a real grower's calendar rather than guessing. TODO(jamie):
  decide whether to keep a simple month-by-month UK seasonality reference in
  `../00-foundations/` so posts stay accurate.

### The why, gently

- The positive case for local, named, in-season flowers. Never the guilt case
  (see the British-grown note in `brand-voice.md`). Sparing.

### Show-the-product, softly

- A clean screen recording of a real grower's page, or a "set yours up in an
  evening" Reel framed as "look how good your work could look". Rare. Never a
  feature demo, never "sign up now".

### Hashtags

Use the real British-flower tag ecosystem. Three to six, lowercase, all genuine:

- Core: `#britishflowers` `#grownnotflown` `#flowersfromthefarm`
  `#seasonalflowers` `#slowflowers`
- Add the relevant local tag per post: county or town (e.g. `#devon`,
  `#yorkshireflowers`). TODO(jamie): build a short list of the strongest regional
  British-flower tags as we feature growers in each area.
- Never hashtag soup, never `#love #flowers #instagood`.

### What we never do

- No engagement bait ("comment your favourite", "tag a florist who needs this").
- No flower puns, anywhere, ever.
- No urgency tricks, countdowns, or "limited spots".
- No emoji clutter. A single functional emoji (a location pin) is the ceiling.
- Never argue the imported-flowers debate in comments. Positive case or silence.
