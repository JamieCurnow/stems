By the middle of summer most growers have the opposite of a sales problem. The
dahlias are coming three times a week, the sweet peas are everywhere, and you've
got more cut than the regular shop can take. The hard part isn't growing it. It's
letting the right people know what you've got, this week, before it's over.

If you sell flowers locally, to florists, at markets, for weddings, or straight
from the gate, here's how to do it well without disappearing down a website
project you don't have time for.

## You probably don't need a website

A proper website is a lovely thing to not have time for. It's weeks of work,
it costs money, and the bit that actually matters, what you have available right
now, is the bit that goes stale fastest. A website that says you grow "seasonal
British flowers" and was last touched in March isn't helping anyone in July.

What you need is much smaller and much more useful: one clear place that shows
what you've got this week, with prices, that you can keep current in a few
minutes and share with a single link. Everything else, the story, the photos,
the about, is nice to have. The live availability is the job.

## Start with where people already find you

Most small growers already have an audience. It's just scattered.

- **Instagram** is where the photos and the goodwill live. It's brilliant for
  showing the field and terrible for "ranunculus, 60p a stem, ready Friday".
- **Your local florists** are your best repeat buyers. They want local stock and
  a story to tell their own customers, and they'd rather text one grower than
  trawl an auction.
- **Markets and the farm gate** bring the public who want a bucket of just-cut
  stems over a bunch flown in from the other side of the world.
- **Word of mouth** in your area, especially through other growers, does more
  than any ad ever will.

You don't need a new audience. You need to make it effortless for the one you've
got to see what's available and reach you.

## Make your availability easy to share

Here's the pattern that works, however you do it technically.

1. **Keep one list, in one place.** Variety, colour, price per stem, and whether
   it's ready now or coming soon. One source of truth, not twelve different
   answers in twelve different chats.
2. **Update it as you cut.** A list is only worth sharing if people trust it's
   current. Touch it when the picture changes, even just to say what's finished.
3. **Share the same link every time.** When someone asks what's in, you send the
   link instead of retyping the list. Put that link at the top of your Instagram,
   in your bio, in your email signature, and on a sign at the market.

The whole trick is replacing "let me write you a list" with "here's my link".
You do the work once, when you cut, and the same link serves the florist on
Monday and the bride's mum on Thursday.

## Price it so it's easy to order

Florists buy by the stem and usually in bunches of ten, so price per stem with a
clear bunch size. Be consistent, and don't undersell British-grown stems to
compete with imported stock on price alone, because you're not selling the same
thing. You're selling flowers that were in a field this morning, with a name and
a season attached. That's worth more, and the people who want it know it.

If you're not sure where to pitch, look at what comparable local growers charge,
factor in your real time and costs, and start there. You can always adjust as
you learn what sells.

## Let new buyers find you, not just your regulars

Sharing a link is how you serve the people who already know you. Being findable
is how you reach the ones who don't yet. A florist new to the area, an event
planner after a specific colour in season, someone who's decided they want local
flowers this year: they're all searching, and right now most of them end up at an
importer because the local grower was invisible online.

Being listed somewhere searchable, by your name and your area, closes that gap.
It turns "I didn't know there was a grower near me" into a message in your inbox.
