# Brand voice (master)

The master voice document. It governs the **blog, SEO content, About page,
landing/marketing pages, email, and any long-form or product-facing writing**.
If a piece of copy doesn't match this, rewrite it.

Short-form social (Instagram captions, Reels, Stories) runs a slightly looser,
warmer register. That lives in `social-voice.md`. The two are siblings, not
rivals: same brand, same beliefs, different room. This doc is the calmer,
considered one. For anything written and long-form, this wins.

Read `positioning.md` first. The voice serves the positioning: we make small
growers look legitimate, and we speak the way the best of them already do.

## The voice in one line

A grower's friend who happens to write well. Warm, unhurried, quietly
confident. Knows flowers and respects the craft, never lectures, never hypes,
never sells hard. The work, and the season, do the talking.

Think the tone of a good independent shop's hand-written sign, or a Toast
catalogue, or Kinfolk without the pretension. Editorial, natural, a little
plain. Never corporate, never "marketplace".

## Core voice rules (do not break)

1. **No em-dashes. Anywhere. Ever.** Commas, full stops, colons, brackets,
   whatever you want. Never an em-dash. (This is also a repo-wide rule, see
   `.agent/rules/copywriting.md`.)
2. **UK spelling and usage throughout.** Colour, realise, neighbour, favourite,
   organise. British seasons, British flowers, British growers.
3. **Warm and clear, never hyped.** No "game-changing," no "revolutionise your
   floristry business," no exclamation marks doing emotional work. The thing is
   nice enough. Say it plainly.
4. **Plain words over jargon.** No "two-sided marketplace," no "supply chain
   solution," no "empowering creators." Say what it is: a page for your flowers
   that you can share. If a flower-world word is the right word (stems, conditioning,
   the Dutch auction, seasonality, British-grown), use it, that's our world.
5. **Short sentences. Let it breathe.** White space on the page mirrors white
   space in the design. Don't crowd.
6. **Lead with the grower and the flowers, not the software.** Open on something
   real: a bucket of just-cut dahlias, the 6am DM pile, a florist asking what's
   in. The product is the calm answer to a real thing, mentioned plainly when
   it's earned. Never open on a feature.
7. **Honest about what it is and isn't.** We don't take a cut on sales in V1,
   we don't get between you and your buyer, we're not the Dutch auction online.
   Say so, calmly. Honesty is the brand's spine, not a disclaimer.
8. **Celebrate the grower, never patronise them.** They know more about flowers
   than we ever will. We're proud to be useful to them. Write like we're lucky
   to have them, because we are.
9. **A light, dry warmth is welcome. Cutesy is not.** No flower puns as a crutch
   ("let's grow together," "blooming brilliant", "thanks a bunch"). A wry, human
   aside is fine. A pun in a headline is a rewrite.

## The promise, and how we talk about it

This is the heart of the brand and it does not change between registers.

**We make you look as good as your flowers, without making you build a website.**

- The story (editorial, the About page, the blog) can be warm and a little
  romantic about the craft: the season turning, the named grower, the field at
  dawn, flowers that haven't been on a plane. Lean into that honestly, it's true
  and it's lovely.
- The moment **the product** enters the sentence (a CTA, a feature line, a
  sign-up prompt), it stays plain and concrete: a shareable page for your
  flowers, with live availability, that you update in minutes and share with one
  link. It does not promise sales, audiences, or success. It gives you a good
  shopfront. What you do with it is yours.

Good (warm story, plain product):

> Your flowers already sell themselves. They just need somewhere to be seen
> that isn't buried in a DM thread. Stems is a single page for what you've got
> this week, with photos, prices, and what's in season, that you share with one
> link and update in the time it takes to have a coffee.

Avoid (hype):

> Stems is the all-in-one platform revolutionising how growers sell flowers
> online. Grow your business, reach more buyers, and skyrocket your sales today!

Avoid (cold / corporate):

> Stems is a two-sided B2B marketplace connecting flower producers with
> commercial buyers via a streamlined availability-management workflow.

The first is the bar. Same product, but it sounds like a person who likes
flowers, not a pitch deck.

## Voice by surface

### About page

- Open on the grower and the problem, not on "Stems is a platform that..."
- Tell the why: local, seasonal, named, the bit of the flower world that got
  flown in and lost its names. Make the case for British-grown without
  sermonising. Respect the reader's intelligence.
- Be clear and proud about what Stems does and doesn't do (no cut, no middleman,
  keeps your customers yours). This is where trust is won.
- End on the invitation: set up your page, put the link in your bio. Concrete,
  warm, no hard sell.

### Blog / SEO

- **Open on the concrete.** A flower, a season, a real question a grower or
  florist actually asks. "By July the sweet peas are coming three times a week
  and you've got more than the shop can take." Not "In this article we explore
  the benefits of local sourcing."
- Be **genuinely useful and genuinely accurate.** Seasonality, varieties,
  conditioning, pricing, the realities of selling locally. Real knowledge, no
  invented stats. If you need a figure (e.g. "% of UK cut flowers imported"),
  pull a real source or leave `TODO(jamie)`. Getting it wrong costs more than a
  dull sentence.
- The Stems mention is **calm and woven in**, usually one paragraph near the
  end, varied post to post (never the same boilerplate). The article earns it.
- Vary the soft CTA. Identical product paragraphs across posts read as
  boilerplate and lower trust.

### Landing / marketing pages

- Read it aloud. Warm, clear, a little understated. If it sounds like it's
  performing, rewrite.
- Headlines short, body short. Let the design and the flowers carry the feeling.
- Speak to the grower first ("List your flowers"), with a quiet door for buyers
  ("or find a grower near you").

### Email

- Sign off `Jamie`. Not "The Stems Team," not "Best, Jamie." Just `Jamie`.
  (Where a from-name is needed it's "Stems," but the human signs off as Jamie.)
- Subject lines warm and specific, not shouty. "your page is ready when you are"
  beats "Get Started With Stems Today!".
- One thing per email. One ask, one link.
- No marketing closers ("We can't wait to see you bloom!"). Human, then stop.

## Word and phrase guide

| Use | Don't use |
|---|---|
| your page, your shopfront, your link | "your storefront", "your listing", "your profile on our platform" |
| growers, flower farmers, the people who grow it | "vendors", "sellers", "suppliers", "producers" |
| florists, event florists, buyers, people who buy local | "customers" (when we mean the grower's customers), "demand-side users" |
| local, seasonal, just-cut, British-grown, in season now | "fresh" (everyone says fresh), "premium", "artisanal" |
| flowers, stems, blooms (sparingly), buckets, bunches | "products", "SKUs", "inventory", "floral assets" |
| share one link, put it in your bio, send it on | "drive traffic", "convert", "acquire users" |
| set up your page, list what you've got | "onboard", "create your seller account" |
| we don't take a cut / we stay out of it | "low fees", "competitive commission" |
| the Dutch auction, imported, airfreighted | (don't sneer at it, just contrast plainly) |

The split that matters: **the story can be romantic about flowers; the product
stays a plain, honest shopfront.** "Flowers that were in a field this morning"
is the dream, write it. "A page for your flowers you can share with one link"
is the product, every time.

## Tagline

**Local-grown flowers, straight from the grower.** (Currently live on
`/discover`. Keep it as the descriptor line.)

Eyebrow / rhythm line, already in the product: **Local · Seasonal · Grown.**

Shorter alternates, only where the descriptor won't fit:
- The shopfront for local growers.
- Straight from the grower.

(If we ever want a single punchy line, candidates to test: "Grown, not flown."
Hold for now, it's good but slightly more combative than the house tone.)

## A note on the British-grown angle

It's our strongest story and our biggest trap. Strongest: it's true, it's
timely, growers and conscious buyers care about it deeply. Trap: it tips easily
into preachy or smug ("stop killing the planet with your imported roses").

The rule: **make the positive case, never the guilt case.** We're for the named
local grower, the turning season, the flower with a story. We're not against the
person buying supermarket tulips in February. Contrast plainly, celebrate
loudly, lecture never.

## When in doubt

Picture the kind of grower we want on Stems explaining it to another grower over
a cup of tea at a market stall. Warm, unhurried, proud of the work, honest about
what it is, never overselling, never talking down. If your copy sounds more
excited than that, or more corporate, or like it's performing, it's wrong.
