# 02: Grower outreach

The most important channel. Read `../00-foundations/positioning.md` first. The
growth loop is grower-to-grower, and this folder is how we start it by hand.

## Why this channel

Stems grows when growers join, share their page, and tell other growers. We
cannot wait for that loop to start on its own. Someone has to find the first ten
to twenty growers and warmly, personally invite them. That is this channel.

This is not advertising. It is the founder turning up in the places British
flower growers already gather, being genuinely useful, and offering a tool that
makes their lives easier with no catch. The pitch is small and honest: a calm
shopfront for your flowers, set up in an evening, no cut on your sales, you keep
your customers. We are lucky to have them, and we write like it.

## Who we reach

Small, freelance, and home British flower growers (see the grower picture in
`positioning.md`). Specifically the ones who:

- Sell to local florists, at markets, from the farm gate, or for weddings.
- Already post on Instagram but have no good way to show live availability.
- Care about seasonality and British-grown, and are allergic to anything corporate.

The single best targets first are growers who are **active, proud, and already
sharing their work**, because they will use the page and tell their peers.

## What good looks like

- 10 to 20 growers personally approached, warmly, one at a time.
- A meaningful share of them set up a live page and put the link in their bio.
- A handful of them tell their Flowers from the Farm group or local grower
  friends, unprompted, because the thing is good and we made them feel proud.

Quality over volume. One delighted grower who tells their county group is worth
more than fifty cold DMs.

How to approach (warm, never spammy):

- Start by **being a real, useful presence**, not by dropping a link. Learn the
  groups, the names, the seasonal rhythm.
- Identify members who are clearly active and proud of their work and who would
  genuinely benefit (no live-availability solution today).
- Reach out **individually and personally**, referencing their actual flowers,
  not a copy-paste blast.

### Local growers' markets and farmers' markets

- Stallholders selling cut flowers are ideal: they are local, proud, and
  re-typing their availability constantly.
- An in-person conversation at a stall ("I love your flowers, I'm building a
  simple page thing for growers, can I show you?") converts far better than any
  DM, and gives us genuine grower features.

### County and regional grower Facebook groups

- Many counties have flower-grower or cut-flower groups beyond FFTF. These are
  where local availability and "anyone got X this week?" conversations already
  happen.
- Same rule: be useful first, never spam, talk to people individually.

## The hook

Keep it small and true. The hook is not "grow your business". It is:

> Set up a page for your flowers in an evening. Photos, prices, what is in this
> week, all on one link you put in your Instagram bio. You update it as you cut.
> No cut on your sales, we stay out of it, you keep your customers. It is free
> to set up and yours to share.

Lead with the relief, not the software: stop re-typing your availability into
twelve separate chats.

## Featuring as the thank-you

The reward for an early grower is not a discount. It is being celebrated. When a
grower sets up, we **feature them** on Instagram and on `/discover`, credited and
by name. That:

- Makes them feel proud and seen (the thing they actually want).
- Sends their own audience to their page.
- Shows every other grower watching what their page could look like.

Featuring early growers is both the thank-you and the next outreach, all at once.

## Outreach templates (Stems voice)

Personalise every one. Reference their actual flowers. Never blast.

### Instagram / Facebook DM (short)

> Hi [name], I love your [specific flowers, e.g. "ranunculus this week"]. I'm
> Jamie, I'm building a simple thing for growers like you: a single page for
> your flowers, with photos, prices and what's in season, that you share with one
> link and update as you cut. It saves you re-typing your list into every DM.
> No cut on your sales, you keep your customers, it's free to set up. Could I
> show you what it looks like? No pressure at all.

### Email (a little longer, still plain)

> Subject: a simple page for your flowers
>
> Hi [name],
>
> I came across your flowers via [where] and they're lovely, the [specific] in
> particular.
>
> I'm building something small for growers and wanted to show you in case it's
> useful. It's a single page for what you've got: photos, prices, what's in
> season this week, all on one link you can put at the top of your Instagram. You
> update it as you cut, in the time it takes to have a coffee. It saves having
> the same "what have you got?" conversation twelve times a week.
>
> A few honest things: there's no cut on your sales (Stems never gets between you
> and your buyer), it's free to set up, and the customers stay yours. It's early,
> and I'm setting up the first growers personally.
>
> If you'd like, I'll set your page up with you in an evening, or just send you
> the link to try. Either way, no pressure.
>
> Jamie

### In-person, at a market (the script, not a recital)

> "I really like your flowers. I'm building a simple page thing for growers, just
> somewhere to show what you've got each week on one link for your bio. Mind if I
> show you on my phone? Takes a second."

## Tone rules for outreach

- One grower, one personal message. If it reads like a template, rewrite it.
- Lead with their flowers, not our product.
- Be honest and small: free to set up, no cut, you keep your customers, it's
  early.
- No follow-up nagging. One warm nudge at most, then leave it.
- If they say no, thank them warmly and move on. The community is small and
  word travels; we are remembered for how we behaved.
